# Arc 0 v3 — Master Specification

بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم

> أَحْسَنُ عَمَلًا — "best in deed" (67:2)

This spec encodes everything learned from v1 (9 shura rounds, 30+ fixes),
v2 (built from spec, 0 fixes needed), and the structural insight that
separates v3 from both: **TENSION → NEED → TOOL → RELIEF**.

---

## The Product

**Al-Khwarizmi's Notebook** — an Islamic A-Level mathematics
curriculum. Arc 0 ("Genesis") is the pre-FP1 foundation.
Everything a student needs before Further Pure Mathematics 1.

**The reader:** A 16-year-old Muslim student. They may be
strong or weak in maths. They are not weak in faith, but
they have never seen their faith and their education as the
same thing. This book changes that.

**The thesis:** Mathematics is not imposed on creation. It is
READ from creation. The Quran generates mathematical problems
through its own commands. To obey — to divide justly, to record
debts, to observe the moon — you must compute. The computation
is not separate from the worship. It IS the worship made precise.

---

## The Core Principle

> **TENSION → NEED → TOOL → RELIEF**
>
> Not: tool → application → reflection.
>
> The reader must WANT the formula before they see it.
> The derivation is the relief after the tension.
> The problem comes from a PERSON whose need the reader inhabits.
> The aha moment belongs to the READER, not the narrator.

This is how the Quran teaches. It does not say "here is the rule
for inheritance." It says "Allah instructs you CONCERNING YOUR
CHILDREN" — it addresses you, places you in the situation, THEN
gives the instruction (4:11). The command follows the context.
The mathematics follows the human need (12:2-3).

Every island must follow this pattern:
1. A PERSON arrives with a NEED the reader can feel
2. The need creates TENSION — the current tools cannot solve it
3. The reader sits with the tension for at least 3-5 paragraphs
   before any formal mathematics appears
4. Al-Khwarizmi builds the TOOL — and the reader feels relief
5. The PRACTICE PROBLEMS are the person's remaining questions
6. The person's outcome is noted — what happened to them?

**If the tool arrives before the tension, the island has failed.**
**If the person vanishes and only math remains, the island has failed.**

## The Voice

First person. "I" — Al-Khwarizmi discovering. Timeless setting
(works in any era, any Muslim city).

### What Al-Khwarizmi sounds like

A person THINKING, not explaining. Terse in discovery.
Shows through ACTION, not narration.

**Best examples from v2 (match this register):**

> "A man has died."

One sentence. Immediate. No setup.

> "I stop. 27/24. That is greater than 1."

Discovery through action. He computes, hits a wall, reacts.

> "The wife is watching me. She has seen the number I wrote.
> She does not read mathematics, but she reads faces, and she
> has read mine."

The person is present. The scene is physical. The tension is FELT.

> "I do not prove this. I cannot. To prove something, I need
> prior truths. This IS the prior truth."

Honest limitation. Direct.

### What Al-Khwarizmi does NOT sound like

**Narrator previewing:**
"This is the principle I will carry through every island."
FIX: Cut it. If the principle carries through, the reader will see it.

**Meta-commentary:**
"Each extension is driven by a problem the previous system could not solve."
FIX: Show the system breaking. Don't describe the pattern.

**Telling the reader what to feel:**
"The mathematics served the family."
FIX: Show the wife standing up. Show the father leaving. Let the reader conclude.

**Choreographing attention:**
"Now one more observation." / "Let me name what I have observed."
FIX: Just observe. Just name. Skip the announcement.

**Claude literary prose:**
"Some truths are asymptotic." / "This is not humiliation. It is calibration."
TEST: Would a real person discovering math for the first time say this? If no, cut.

**Philosophizing:**
"Simplicity reflects Tawhid — unnecessary complexity is a form of shirk."
TEST: Is the narrator explaining or discovering? If explaining, rewrite as discovery.

### The two voice tests (apply to every paragraph)

**Test A — Would a real person say this?**
A mathematician discovering something for the first time tries things,
gets stuck, has insights, moves on. They do not narrate their emotional
state, build dramatic tension artificially, or editorialize about later work.

**Test B — Is the narrator showing or telling?**
TELLING: "The distributive law connects addition and multiplication."
SHOWING: "7 fields, 100 good ears and 20 damaged per field. I need
7 × 100 + 7 × 20. But 7 × 120 gives the same number. Why?"

## The Tension Map

This is the structural core of v3. Every island's tension is defined
BEFORE building. The gap between TENSION and AHA is where
understanding lives.

### Prologue — The Impossible Sum

| | |
|---|---|
| **Person** | A dead man's family: wife, two daughters, father, mother |
| **Need** | Just division of the estate |
| **Tension** | The Quran prescribes exact shares: 1/8 + 2/3 + 1/6 + 1/6 = 27/24 > 1. The shares demand MORE than the estate. Al-Khwarizmi cannot solve it — he doesn't have fractions, equations, or even a definition of "equal." |
| **Aha** | None yet. The prologue IS the tension. It exists to make the reader feel the weight of a problem they cannot solve. |
| **Outcome** | Al-Khwarizmi commits: build every tool from 1 = 1. |

### 0.1 Equality — The Scales

| | |
|---|---|
| **Person** | A buyer in the souk who suspects the merchant's weights are false |
| **Physical** | Saffron on one pan, brass on the other. The beam tilts. |
| **Need** | Is this scale true? Who is right — the buyer or the merchant? |
| **Tension** | Al-Khwarizmi has just seen the inheritance problem. He sits down to work. He writes "Wife's share = 1/8." Then stops. What does "=" mean? If he doesn't know what equality IS, every equation he writes rests on nothing. He goes to the souk to watch a scale — the physical embodiment of "=". But the buyer and merchant are arguing. The buyer says the weights are false. Both men stare at the scale. What makes a scale TRUE? |
| **Aha** | A true scale has three properties: reflexive, symmetric, transitive. Plus: what you do to one side, you do to the other (preservation). These ARE the definition of equality. |
| **Where tension breaks** | The formal naming of properties should not begin until Al-Khwarizmi has struggled with the question "what makes this scale true" through at least 3-5 paragraphs of TRYING to understand. He watches weighings. He swaps pans. He chains comparisons. THEN he names what he's found. |
| **Outcome** | The buyer and merchant agree — because the scale is true. Al-Khwarizmi has his first tool. |
| **Pearson test** | Reflexive/symmetric/transitive appears in every textbook. What makes this different: the scale IS the equality relation made physical. The verse (21:47) IS about scales. The connection is structural, not decorative. The buyer's dispute drives the discovery — you don't get this in Pearson. |

### 0.2 Counting — The Shepherd's Gate

| | |
|---|---|
| **Person** | A shepherd bringing his flock through the city gate at dusk |
| **Physical** | Staff in hand, touches each animal as it passes. His dog limps — injured, can't chase strays. |
| **Need** | None lost, none miscounted. One missing = ruin. |
| **Tension** | Al-Khwarizmi has equality but nothing to BE equal. He can write "a = b" but what are a and b? He needs numbers. He watches the shepherd. Each animal through the gate is "one more." But what guarantees the process never stops? What guarantees that after 47 there IS a 48? And if one sheep is sick and doesn't come through — how does the shepherd know one is MISSING unless counting is exhaustive? |
| **Aha** | The successor function: after every number, exactly one next number. Natural numbers. Addition as repeated succession. The induction axiom: if something is true for 1, and being true for n means it's true for n+1, then it's true forever. The shepherd's method IS induction — check each one, and the next, and the next. |
| **Where tension breaks** | The formal definition of natural numbers and the successor function should not appear until the reader has felt the shepherd's anxiety: what if one is missing? What if I miscount? What guarantees counting works? |
| **Outcome** | 47 went out. 47 came back. None lost, none miscounted. |

### 0.3 Multiplication — The Farmer's Grain

| | |
|---|---|
| **Person** | A farmer at the masjid, needing his harvest total for zakat |
| **Physical** | Dusty hands, grain samples in cloth bags, worried about the deadline |
| **Need** | He has 7 fields. Each yields roughly 100 measures. Zakat (2.5%) is due at the harvest. How much does he owe? |
| **Tension** | He could add: 100 + 100 + 100 + 100 + 100 + 100 + 100. That's slow but works for 7 fields. But his neighbor has 23 fields each yielding 47 measures. Adding 47 twenty-three times is miserable. And two of the farmer's fields had damaged crops — 80 measures instead of 100. Now it's 5 × 100 + 2 × 80. Is that the same as 7 × 100 minus something? He can't tell. His tool (addition) works but it's SLOW and FRAGILE for real problems. |
| **Aha** | Multiplication as repeated addition — with properties that make it MORE than a shortcut. Commutative: 7 × 100 = 100 × 7 (whether you count fields of grain or grains per field, same total). Distributive: 7 × (100 − 20) = 7 × 100 − 7 × 20 (the damaged-field shortcut). Associative: grouping doesn't matter. |
| **Where tension breaks** | Let the farmer struggle. Let the reader feel the tedium of adding 47 twenty-three times. Let the damaged-field problem create genuine confusion. THEN introduce multiplication as the relief. |
| **Outcome** | The farmer's total: 5 × 100 + 2 × 80 = 660 measures. Zakat: 660/40 = 16.5 measures. But wait — he can't compute that division yet. He needs fractions. (Forward bridge to 0.5.) |

### 0.4 Zero & Negatives — The Debtor's Position

| | |
|---|---|
| **Person** | A debtor standing before the qadi (judge) |
| **Physical** | Three dinars in his hand. A contract showing he owes five. The qadi's pen is raised, waiting to record. |
| **Need** | What is his honest position? "I have 3, I owe 5" — but what is 3 − 5? |
| **Tension** | The qadi asks: "What is your total position?" The debtor says "I owe 2." The qadi reaches for his pen, then stops. He needs to WRITE a number. But 3 − 5 = ? There is no counting number for this. The natural numbers start at 1. There is nothing below 1. The number system has a HOLE where this man's honest position should be. The qadi cannot record justice because the mathematics cannot express debt. |
| **Aha** | Zero: the number that means "nothing owed, nothing owned." The additive identity — add it to anything, nothing changes. Negatives: numbers below zero. −2 means "owing 2." The integers extend the naturals in both directions. Now 3 − 5 = −2. The qadi can write. And (−1)(−1) = 1: cancelling a debt IS a gain. |
| **Where tension breaks** | The qadi's inability to write — SIT with that. The pen is raised. The number doesn't exist. The reader should feel the absurdity: this man's position is REAL but the math can't say it. THEN zero and negatives arrive as the fix. |
| **Outcome** | The qadi writes: "Position: −2 dinars." The debtor's honest position is recorded. Justice requires naming debt, not hiding it. |

### 0.5 Fractions — The Inheritance Fulfilled

| | |
|---|---|
| **Person** | The grieving family from the prologue: wife, two daughters, father, mother |
| **Physical** | The wife leads. Steady. Two daughters walk close together. The father enters last, slow — burying his son. The mother is already seated, waiting. |
| **Need** | Just division of 2700 dinars according to the Quran's exact shares |
| **Tension** | Al-Khwarizmi has integers, addition, multiplication, zero, negatives. The family sits before him. The Quran prescribes: wife 1/8, daughters 2/3, father 1/6, mother 1/6. But "1/8" is not an integer. It lives BETWEEN the whole numbers. His tools break at the threshold of this family's need. He has spent four islands building tools for whole numbers. Not one of them can handle "one-eighth." |
| **Aha** | Fractions as multiplicative inverses. Common denominators to add them. Then the devastating discovery: 3/24 + 16/24 + 4/24 + 4/24 = 27/24 > 1. The shares exceed the estate. The method of 'awl — proportional reduction — restores balance without destroying any relationship the Quran established. |
| **Where tension breaks** | The family must be present and vivid BEFORE any fraction arithmetic begins. The reader should feel what the family needs. Then the tools fail. Then fractions. Then the SECOND tension: 27/24 > 1 — which should hit like a punch. Then 'awl as the second relief. |
| **Outcome** | Wife: 300 dinars. Each daughter: 800. Father: 400. Mother: 400. Total: 2700. Every dinar accounted for. The wife stands first. She gathers her daughters. |
| **Pearson test** | No Pearson textbook teaches fractions through Islamic inheritance with 'awl. The legal problem DRIVES the math. The 27/24 > 1 overflow is unique to this curriculum. The Quran IS the problem set. |

### 0.6 Variables — The Merchant's Unknown

| | |
|---|---|
| **Person** | A broad-shouldered merchant, smelling of sandalwood and sea salt, ink-stained fingers |
| **Physical** | He keeps his own ledgers. His ships are at sea. His warehouses are uncounted. |
| **Need** | He owes zakat — 1/40 of his total wealth. He knows the RULE. He does not know the NUMBER. |
| **Tension** | Every tool Al-Khwarizmi has built works on KNOWN numbers. Add 3 + 5. Multiply 7 × 100. Compute 1/8 of 2700. But this merchant's wealth is UNKNOWN. His ships haven't returned. His warehouses haven't been counted. He knows he owes 1/40 of his wealth, but he can't compute 1/40 of something he doesn't have. He needs to write an equation with a number he doesn't know yet. The tools break — not because they're wrong, but because they require KNOWN inputs. |
| **Aha** | The variable: shay' (thing). Call the unknown x. Write: zakat = x/40. Now manipulate the equation without knowing x. When information arrives — a ship returns, a warehouse is counted — substitute and solve. The unknown becomes known. |
| **Where tension breaks** | Let the merchant describe his situation in full. Let the reader feel the impossibility: you owe a specific amount, computed from a specific rule, applied to a number you DON'T HAVE. THEN introduce the variable as the way to work with what you don't know. |
| **Outcome** | Al-Khwarizmi writes the merchant's equations. When the first ship returns with 800 dinars of goods, the equation updates: x is at least 800, zakat is at least 20. The merchant can begin paying before he knows the total. |

### 0.7 Quadratics — The Patron's Garden

| | |
|---|---|
| **Person** | A wealthy patron standing in his courtyard |
| **Physical** | Square garden, 10 cubits per side. He wants to expand. |
| **Need** | He wants to add strips of equal width to two adjacent sides, doubling the area. How wide must the strips be? |
| **Tension** | "My garden is 10 by 10. I want the area to become 200. I will add a strip of width x to the east and south sides. What is x?" Al-Khwarizmi writes: (10 + x)(10 + x) = 200. Expands: x² + 20x + 100 = 200. Simplifies: x² + 20x = 100. He stares at it. This is NOT like the merchant's equation. The unknown is SQUARED. x appears in two places, one of them multiplied by itself. The linear tools from 0.6 — isolate x, divide both sides — don't work. He can't isolate x because it's tangled with itself. |
| **Aha** | Completing the square — geometrically. Al-Khwarizmi DRAWS the garden. The x² is a small square. The 20x is two rectangles. He rearranges them into an almost-complete larger square and fills in the missing corner. The geometry SOLVES the algebra. The quadratic formula emerges from a picture, not from symbol manipulation. |
| **Where tension breaks** | Let Al-Khwarizmi TRY linear methods. Let them fail. Let the reader see that x² + 20x = 100 resists every tool from 0.6. THEN the geometric insight. The completion is VISUAL before it's algebraic. |
| **Outcome** | x = −10 + √200 = −10 + 10√2 ≈ 4.14 cubits. The patron begins his expansion. But √2 appeared — a number that is not a fraction. That's the next island. |
| **Pearson test** | Pearson teaches completing the square as algebra. Al-Khwarizmi's original method (Kitab al-Jabr wa'l-Muqabala) IS geometric completion. This island teaches the HISTORICAL method — geometry first, formula second. The story is not wallpaper. It's how al-jabr was actually invented. |

### 0.8 Irrationals — The Builder's Diagonal

| | |
|---|---|
| **Person** | A builder measuring a courtyard for a tile order |
| **Physical** | Calloused hands, chalk dust, measuring cord stretched across the diagonal |
| **Need** | The diagonal length — exact, not approximate — so he can order the right number of tiles |
| **Tension** | The courtyard is a perfect square, 1 cubit per side. The builder stretches his cord across the diagonal. He measures: about 1 cubit and 2/5. He writes 7/5. Checks: (7/5)² = 49/25 = 1.96. Not 2. He tries 141/100. Checks: (141/100)² = 19881/10000 = 1.9881. Not 2. He tries 1414/1000. Still not 2. He tries fraction after fraction. NONE square to exactly 2. The number EXISTS — the cord has a definite length, he can hold it in his hands. But he CANNOT WRITE it as a fraction. His number system has a hole where this measurement should be. |
| **Aha** | Proof by contradiction: assume √2 = a/b in lowest terms, derive that both a and b must be even — contradiction. √2 is NOT rational. The real numbers ℝ extend Q. The number line is denser than fractions alone. The Quran commanded: "Do they not look at how it is created?" (88:17). He looked. He found something his tools couldn't hold. So he built new tools. |
| **Where tension breaks** | Let the builder TRY fractions. Let the reader try with him. 7/5? No. 99/70? No. 577/408? No. The repeated failure IS the tension. The reader should feel: "there MUST be a fraction that works" — and then the proof shows there isn't. That's the aha. |
| **Outcome** | The builder orders tiles for a diagonal of √2 cubits. The number can be approximated as closely as needed, but never written exactly as a fraction. His courtyard is built. |

### 0.9 Functions — The Muezzin's Rule

| | |
|---|---|
| **Person** | Bilal, the aging muezzin, retiring after 40 years |
| **Physical** | Weathered hands, prayer-callused knees, standing on the minaret at sunset |
| **Need** | His successor Yusuf needs Bilal's knowledge written down |
| **Tension** | Bilal has called every prayer for 40 years. He watches the sun, the shadows, the stars. He KNOWS when to call — it's in his bones. But Yusuf asks: "How will I know?" Bilal tries: "When the shadow of the post equals the post's height, it is Asr." Yusuf writes this down. But there are five prayers, and the shadow rule changes with the season. In summer the days are long; in winter they are short. Bilal has dozens of rules, accumulated over decades. Some are for the sun, some for the stars, some for the color of the sky. He can't just hand Yusuf his experience. He needs a way to write: "given THIS input, the output is THAT." A RULE that turns any time-of-year into a prayer time. Something Yusuf can follow without 40 years of watching. |
| **Aha** | A function: input → rule → output. f(day) = prayer_time. Domain: the set of valid inputs. Codomain: the set of possible outputs. Range: the outputs that actually occur. Types: some functions are one-to-one, some are many-to-one. Composition: chaining functions (first compute shadow length from sun angle, then compute prayer time from shadow length). |
| **Where tension breaks** | Let Bilal try to explain WITHOUT functions. Let the reader feel the mess: dozens of rules, special cases, seasonal adjustments. The knowledge is real but untransmittable. THEN the function formalizes what Bilal already knows — and suddenly Yusuf can compute any prayer time for any day. |
| **Outcome** | Bilal writes his functions. Yusuf reads them. For the first time, Yusuf calls the Asr prayer on his own — not by watching Bilal, but by computing. The function is the bridge between the master's experience and the student's obedience. |
| **Pearson test** | Pearson teaches functions with f(x) = 2x + 3 and domain/range exercises. This island teaches functions as KNOWLEDGE TRANSFER — how do you encode a lifetime of observation into a rule someone else can use? The muezzin's problem gives functions PURPOSE beyond symbol manipulation. |

### 0.10 Limits — The Traveler's Approach

| | |
|---|---|
| **Person** | A traveler on foot, approaching a walled city, water running low |
| **Physical** | Dusty cloak, cracked lips, empty water-skin, the city walls visible but distant |
| **Need** | Will he reach the city before his water runs out? |
| **Tension** | The traveler can see the walls. He marks his distance at each milestone. At noon: 8 miles. At 1pm: 4 miles. At 2pm: 2 miles. At 3pm: 1 mile. Every hour, the distance halves. He computes the next few: 1/2 mile, 1/4 mile, 1/8 mile... The distance is ALWAYS positive. It NEVER reaches zero. Yet the walls are getting closer. He can see the gate. He CAN reach it — any fool can see he's almost there. But the MATHEMATICS says the distance is always positive. The math and the reality disagree. Who is right? |
| **Aha** | The limit. The distance APPROACHES zero. lim_{n→∞} 8/2ⁿ = 0. The total distance covered: 8 + 4 + 2 + 1 + 1/2 + ... = 16. The series CONVERGES. He arrives in finite time. The math and reality AGREE — but only when you have the concept of a limit. Without it, "always positive" and "reaches zero" seem contradictory. With it, "approaches zero" is precise. Also: continuity (if f is continuous and changes sign, it crosses zero — the traveler DOES cross the threshold), IVT as the formal guarantee. |
| **Where tension breaks** | Let the reader compute the shrinking distances. Let them feel the paradox: always positive, yet he arrives. The resolution (limits) should arrive only after the reader has sat with the apparent contradiction. |
| **Outcome** | The traveler reaches the city. He drinks. The mathematical resolution: the series converges to 16 miles total. He was always going to arrive. The limit proves what his feet already knew. |

### 0.11 Derivatives — The Fajr Call

| | |
|---|---|
| **Person** | Young Yusuf, the new muezzin, alone on the roof at fajr for the first time |
| **Physical** | On the minaret in the pre-dawn dark. The eastern horizon is a thin line of grey. |
| **Need** | When exactly to call the fajr prayer — the moment true dawn breaks |
| **Tension** | Bilal's function (from 0.9) tells Yusuf the light level at each moment. He can look it up: at 4:00am, this much light. At 4:15, more. At 4:30, more still. But the function gives him VALUES, not RATES. Yusuf doesn't need "how much light is there at 4:15." He needs "how FAST is the light changing at 4:15?" Because fajr is the moment the light SURGES — the transition from slow brightening to rapid dawn. He needs the rate of change at a specific instant. But rate = change / time. At a single instant, the time interval is zero. Change / zero is what the qadi learned in 0.4: undefined. His tools break at the threshold of the question "how fast, right now?" |
| **Aha** | The derivative. Compute the average rate of change over a shrinking interval. Take the LIMIT (from 0.10) as the interval approaches zero. That limit IS the instantaneous rate of change. f'(t) = lim_{h→0} [f(t+h) − f(t)] / h. Power rule via binomial theorem. d/dx notation. The derivative IS the speedometer: not where you are, but how fast you're going. |
| **Where tension breaks** | Let Yusuf TRY to answer "how fast" with current tools. Average rate over an hour — too rough. Over a minute — better. Over a second — better still. But he needs the rate at an INSTANT, and the fraction keeps having zero in the denominator. THEN the limit resolves it. |
| **Outcome** | Yusuf computes the rate of change of light. At the moment it crosses the threshold — the surge of true dawn — he calls the prayer. His first fajr call, exactly on time. The tools from eleven islands — from 1 = 1 through derivative — have brought a young man to his duty. |
| **Pearson test** | Pearson teaches derivatives with f(x) = x² and the limit definition. This island teaches derivatives as RATE OF CHANGE AT AN INSTANT — motivated by a worship need (calling the prayer at the right moment). The physical meaning (how fast is the sky changing?) precedes the formula. The fajr call is not decoration — it IS the derivative. |

## What v2 Got Wrong (do not repeat)

1. **Tension gap missing in 0.1-0.4.** Al-Khwarizmi OBSERVED people and NAMED
   properties. Pattern: observation → naming → formalization. The reader was
   never stuck. v3 must follow: stuck → trying → failing → insight → relief.
   The math must not arrive before the reader wants it.

2. **People in 0.8-0.11 still abstract.** v2's muezzin and traveler are better
   than v1's "anyone watching the moon" but their tension isn't sharp. v3 gives
   every person a PHYSICAL detail, a clear NEED, and a moment where the current
   tools FAIL them specifically.

3. **Section 2 was too smooth.** When Al-Khwarizmi discovers without friction —
   watches a scale, names a property, watches another, names another — the reader
   learns nothing. Discovery requires FAILED attempts. "I try. It fails." is
   stronger than "I observe. I name."

4. **Voice still had Claude moments.** "This is the principle I will carry through
   every island" — previewing. "Each extension is driven by a problem the previous
   system could not solve" — meta-commentary. "The mathematics served the family" —
   telling. v3 cuts all of these. SHOW, don't tell.

5. **Person vanished from practice problems.** Some islands had generic algebra
   in the exercises. v3 requires: the first 2-3 problems in each tier must be
   things the PERSON in the island actually needs computed.

6. **Pearson-identical sections.** Some islands, stripped of Islamic framing,
   read like standard textbook content with a story wrapper. v3 must pass
   Test 6: the story must CHANGE what is taught, how it is taught, or why.
   If the math is identical to Pearson, the story is wallpaper.

7. **Transitions teased the person but not their tension.** "Outside the souk,
   a shepherd brings his flock" — this introduces a person. v3 transitions
   must tease the PROBLEM: "I have equality. But nothing to be equal WITH."
